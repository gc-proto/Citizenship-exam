Regulatory Interpretation Policy

An interpretation policy is an overarching document that outlines the commitments, practices, and tools to be applied by a department or agency when providing Canadians and businesses with information and guidance on regulatory obligations. It also identifies the conditions under which written responses to questions will be provided.

Departmental Context

Citizenship and Immigration Canada (CIC) manages one of the most generous immigration systems in the world. To support this system, the department engages in a number of regulated activities relating to the selection of foreign nationals as temporary and permanent residents; refugee claims; Canada’s admissibility policy and the conditions for entering and remaining in Canada; and screening potential permanent and temporary residents to protect the health, safety, and security of Canadians. CIC is also responsible for passport regulations and regulations made under the Citizenship Act.

CIC is committed to ensuring that stakeholders, including applicants, representatives, non-governmental organization representatives, and the general public, easily understand what they need to do to comply with federal regulatory requirements.

CIC developed this Regulatory Interpretation Policy Statement to provide clear and consistent information on how regulatory compliance guidance is communicated verbally or in writing to stakeholders.

Predictability

Commitment to plain language

  • CIC is committed to providing our stakeholders with publicly available regulatory information and guidance in clear and plain language.

Providing guidance and creating awareness

  • CIC provides regulatory guidance and promotes stakeholder awareness of regulatory requirements through operational manuals, operational bulletins, web portals, application forms and guides, and funding guides, which are available online.

Responding to questions

  • CIC’s service standard for responding to verbal and written requests for regulatory guidance is 20 business days. Requests can be made by telephone, mail, or e-mail. Guidance provided is for informational purposes and is not legally binding.

Service

Service commitment

  • CIC is committed to service excellence and professionalism, and maintains service standards for immigration, citizenship, passport, and grant and contribution programs.

Service accountability

  • CIC will draw on existing mechanisms to track stakeholder problems and concerns, as well as the steps taken to resolve them. These mechanisms include direct contact with CIC through telephone and email, online requests for feedback, as well as various in-person stakeholder forums and consultations.

Staff training

  • CIC is committed to providing accurate and consistent regulatory advice, and will ensure that our officers are trained to respond to stakeholder inquiries in a timely, clear, consistent, and professional manner.

Stakeholder engagement

Commitment to stakeholder engagement

  • CIC will work with stakeholders, as appropriate, when developing, revising or refining regulatory practices and material in order to provide clear and consistent information and guidance on departmental regulations.

Stakeholder engagement mechanisms

  • CIC engages Canadians on the review and development of regulatory information and guidance through in-person consultations, as well as the publication of notices, discussion papers, and other information which is open to public review and comment, and available online.

Improvements

  • Through an online call for feedback and routine stakeholder consultations, CIC will consult with stakeholders by March 31, 2015, to identify specific areas of improvement in the provision of regulatory guidance.
  • Based on stakeholder input, CIC will post priority areas for improvement on our website by March 31, 2015, along with an implementation plan.
  • Identified areas for improvement will be implemented between April 1, 2015, and March 31, 2017. A progress report on the implementation of the Regulatory Interpretation Policy and identified areas for improvement will be released by March 31, 2017.

Resources

Policies, operational instructions and agreements

CIC maintains a list of key policies, operational instructions, and agreements, which are available online to the public. This includes access to operational manuals and bulletins, which CIC and CBSA employees consult for guidance in the exercise of their functions and in applying the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act, the Citizenship Act and their Regulations.

Community partners

CIC also has information and guidance for community partners on the integration of newcomers into Canadian society available online. Information, handbooks, and best practices are available to a number of stakeholders, including educational institutions, immigrant serving organizations, and representatives.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

The FAQs below are meant to provide Canadians and businesses with basic information about Citizenship and Immigration Canada’s (CIC) key regulations. Policies and programs related to these Regulations are directly managed by CIC.

Information related to regulations managed by the Canadian Border Services Agency (CBSA) and the Immigration and Refugee Board (IRB) may be accessed through the Government of Canada website.

Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations (SOR/2002-227)

1. What is the purpose of these regulations?

The purpose of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations (IRPR) is to establish detailed laws, based on authorities and the more general framework provided in the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act, on a range of immigration issues relating to the selection of immigrants, examinations, permanent resident status, admissibility, and referral of refugee claims to the Immigration and Refugee Board.

2. What are the key elements of these regulations?

CIC undertakes a number of key activities under the IRPR. We select foreign nationals as permanent and temporary residents. We offer protection to refugees. CIC develops Canada’s admissibility policy and sets the conditions for entering and remaining in Canada. CIC also conducts, in collaboration with its partners, the screening of potential permanent and temporary residents to protect the health, safety and security of Canadians. Fundamentally, the Department builds a stronger Canada by helping immigrants and refugees settle and fully integrate into Canadian society and the economy.

3. How do these regulations affect Canadian businesses?

Canadian businesses that wish to employ foreign nationals may be required to pay service fees, privilege fees, or other charges, and may also be required to undertake certain administrative requirements.

4. What is the timeline for implementation?

These regulations came into force on June 28, 2002, and are amended as required.

5. Where can I get more information?

More information may be obtained by contacting regs@cic.gc.ca.

Regulations Designating a Body for the Purposes of Paragraph 91(2)(c) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (SOR/2011-142)

1. What is the purpose of these regulations?

The intent of the Regulations is to better protect applicants and enhance public confidence in the immigration system by designating a regulator of immigration consultants that has demonstrated the ability to establish the necessary competence, integrity, accountability, viability and good governance to effectively regulate immigration consultants.

2. What are the key elements of this regulation?

The Regulations designate the Immigration Consultants of Canada Regulatory Council (ICCRC) as the regulator of immigration consultants, whose members in good standing may represent or advise a person for consideration — or offer to do so — in connection with a proceeding or application under the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act.

3. How does this regulation affect Canadian businesses?

These regulations have no impact on Canadian businesses.

4. What is the timeline for implementation?

These regulations came into force on June 30, 2011, and are amended as required.

5. Where can I get more information?

More information may be obtained by contacting regs@cic.gc.ca.

Citizenship Regulations (SOR/93-246)

1. What is the purpose of these regulations?

The purpose of these regulations is to establish legally binding processes and procedures, based on authorities provided in the Citizenship Act, relating to grants, proof, renunciation, resumption and revocation of Canadian citizenship.

2. What are the key elements of this regulation?

Key elements of these regulations include the factors of consideration for grants of citizenship for adopted persons; the criteria related to the official language and knowledge requirements of citizenship; the procedures for citizenship ceremonies, including the oath of citizenship and the processes around replacement, surrender and cancellation of citizenship certificates. The regulations also prescribe the factors the Minister considers in determining whether a hearing may be required relating to revocation of citizenship, and contains information-sharing provisions which help to combat fraud, strengthen program integrity and determine whether citizenship applicants meet the requirements of the Citizenship Act.

3. How does this regulation affect Canadian businesses?

These regulations have no impact on Canadian businesses.

4. What is the timeline for implementation?

These regulations came into force on May 11, 1993, and are amended as required.

5. Where can I get more information?

More information may be obtained by contacting regs@cic.gc.ca.

Citizenship Regulations, No. 2 (SOR/2015-124)

1. What is the purpose of these regulations?

The purpose of these regulations is to establish legally binding application processes and procedures, based on authorities provided in the Citizenship Act, relating to grants, proof, renunciation, resumption, and revocation of Canadian citizenship. The regulations also designate the Immigration Consultants of Canada Regulatory Council (ICCRC) as the regulator of citizenship consultants, whose members in good standing may represent or advise a person for consideration — or offer to do so — in connection with a proceeding or application under the Citizenship Act.

2. What are the key elements of this regulation?

Key elements of these regulations relate to application procedures, including the prescribed form and manner in which applications are to be made and notices are to be given. The regulations also specify the evidence to be provided with applications to be granted, renounce and resume citizenship. In addition, the regulations specify the manner in which an application must be made for the issuance of original and replacement certificates of citizenship, and the delivery of revocation notices. Taken together, these processes and procedures help to facilitate the acquisition, reacquisition or renunciation of Canadian citizenship.

These regulations also designate the body that regulates citizenship consultants.

3. How does this regulation affect Canadian businesses?

These regulations have no impact on Canadian businesses.

4. What is the timeline for implementation?

These regulations came into force on May 28, 2015.

5. Where can I get more information?

More information may be obtained by contacting regs@cic.gc.ca.

Passport and Other Travel Document Services Fees Regulations (SOR/2012-253)

1. What is the purpose of these regulations?

The purpose of these regulations is to update Passport Canada’s fee structure.

2. What are the key elements of this regulation?

These regulations establish new fees for all travel documents and passport services. As of July 1, 2013, Canadian adults residing in Canada will have the option of requesting a 5 or 10 year ePassport for $120 and $160 respectively. Children’s ePassports will be issued for 5 years at $57 for applicants residing in Canada.

3. How does this regulation affect Canadian businesses?

These regulations have no impact on Canadian businesses.

4. What is the timeline for implementation?

Certain provisions came into force on July 1, 2013, and the remaining provisions came into force on March 31, 2014.

5. Where can I get more information?

More information may be obtained by contacting regs@cic.gc.ca.

Call for Feedback on CIC’s Regulatory Guidance

CIC is launching a call for feedback on the regulatory guidance that we provide under the Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations and the Citizenship Regulations. CIC provides regulatory guidance to the public in the form of operational manuals and bulletins, web portals, application forms and guides, and funding guides.

The objective of this call for feedback is to check-in with stakeholders and the public to determine which types of regulatory guidance work well, and which types of regulatory guidance need improvement. In particular, we are seeking feedback on two key questions:

  • How well do CIC’s guidance documents explain what you must do to comply with our regulations?
  • What can be done to improve the way that CIC provides regulatory guidance?

Identifying stakeholder issues through this call for feedback will help CIC to ensure that it develops effective and workable guidance documents. Stakeholder feedback is welcome until March 31, 2015, at which time identified areas of improvement will be posted on the Government of Canada website. By March 31, 2017, a progress report on the implementation of identified areas of improvement will be made available on-line.

Overview of Consultation Activities

Official On-line Call for Feedback

  • As part of its overall regulatory consultation process, an on-line call for feedback on CIC’s regulatory guidance was posted in October 2014.
  • Although no comments have been received by the department to date, CIC will continue to monitor regs@cic.gc.ca and address any future feedback, as appropriate, and for possible incorporation in subsequent updates to the Regulatory Interpretation Policy Statement.

International Mobility Program

  • Since April 1, 2014, Citizenship and Immigration Canada (CIC) has undertaken a number of consultations on the International Mobility Program and the Temporary Foreign Worker Program (TFWP), to discuss the impact of program integrity and compliance reforms on program operations. CIC and Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) are jointly responsible for the TFWP, while CIC is responsible for the International Mobility Program.
  • CIC officials engaged a number of key stakeholders as part of its consultations, including employer associations, government partners, legal professionals, representatives from the oil and gas industry operating offshore, film/TV, commercial production and the visual effects sector, the performing arts sector, accommodation and food services, as well as the ski and snowboard industry, among others.
  • This engagement has taken place through a number of forums, such as webinars, cross-country stakeholder roundtables, bilateral meetings and correspondence.
  • CIC and ESDC officials developed and publicly posted a discussion paper on proposed regulatory changes to enhance the International Mobility Program and TFWP compliance framework, including a new Administrative Monetary Penalties (AMPs) regime, on September 25, 2014. Interested parties were invited to provide input until October 16, 2014.

Application Processing under the Express Entry System

  • On January 1, 2015, CIC implemented its new Express Entry system – an electronic, automated application management system that allows skilled foreign nationals to apply for permanent residence under select economic immigration programs.
  • Officials launched consultations with employers, provinces and territories, and other stakeholders in the fall of 2012, and in 2013, CIC established a nine-member Employer Technical Reference Group, which includes the Canadian Chamber of Commerce, the Canadian Federation of Independent Businesses, the Canadian Construction Association, and the Information and Communication Technology Council.
  • In partnership with provincial and territorial governments, senior CIC officials hosted information sessions with employers across the country from April to June, and October to December 2014.
  • In 2014, CIC officials conducted extensive testing of the Express Entry application system in order to better understand the client experience and enhance the provision of application guidance and instructions.

Key Themes

  • Several reoccurring themes emerged from stakeholder consultations:

International Mobility Program

Clarity of Program Requirements

  • Given the scope of changes to the International Mobility Program, and varying levels of awareness, stakeholders stressed the need to better inform and educate stakeholders and the public on the new program requirements. Stakeholders emphasized the need for clear, simple and consistent program information.

Program Flexibility

  • Several stakeholders noted that they are seeking consideration and accommodation of unique circumstances in various sectors, occupations, and regions. For example, stakeholders highlighted the importance of program flexibility and adaptability for sectors or industries where specific skills or staffing needs could not be met through Canada’s domestic labour supply (for example, in fields requiring highly specialized knowledge).

International Mobility Program Fees on Employer-Specific and Open Work Permits

  • Under the International Mobility Program, CIC implemented a $230 regulatory charge on employers who provide an offer of employment to LMIA exempt employer-specific work permit applicants that will be used to establish an employer inspection and compliance regime for the program. A $100 privilege fee on open work permit applicants was also introduced which will support activities to improve labour market research, promote reciprocal work opportunities for Canadians, and encourage transitions to permanent residence by open work permit holders. In addition, several employers indicated that they would support the introduction of a reasonable fee to support compliance or audit activities.

Enhanced Compliance Framework

  • Stakeholders and employers indicated broad support for enhanced compliance measures to ensure appropriate consequences for employers who misuse the International Mobility Program and the TFWP, including AMPs and bans of various lengths. Employer representatives stated that some of the proposed penalties may be too strong and urged officials to embed procedural fairness throughout the inspection process leading up to a potential AMP or ban. Employers also suggested that the names of non-compliant employers only be published in very serious cases. Comments received were taken into consideration in the development of the proposed regulatory amendments.

Application Processing under the Express Entry System

Faster Processing, Easy to Use System

  • Stakeholders were receptive to the Express Entry system, but expressed a desire for faster processing. Electronic applications are a key factor in addressing this concern along with a reliable, easy-to-use system, and this was highlighted throughout the consultations.

Improvement Priorities and Metrics

  • To address issues identified during consultations, CIC officials will undertake the following activities:

International Mobility Program

  1. Publish Program Delivery Instructions that will clarify program requirements;
  2. Continue engaging stakeholders in various outreach activities to explain program changes (e.g. organize webinars); and
  3. Introduce AMPs and varied ban periods to address employer non-compliance with program requirements while ensuring that procedural fairness is built into the process.

Application Processing under the Express Entry System

  1. Through CIC’s automated electronic application management system, provide clear web-based instructions to clients on how to apply to programs under Express Entry;
  2. Reduce administrative burden through a paperless system that eliminates the need to return paper applications to clients, and which reduces the need to manage and transfer paper files between CIC offices;
  3. Solicit client feedback on the application process so as to identify and resolve issues, and ensure that the system remains efficient and easy to use; and
  4. Establish an application processing service standard of six months for 80% of application, down from 10 to 12 months under the previous system.

Metrics to Assess Implementation

  • To assess the implementation of its interpretation practices, CIC will engage in a number of activities.

International Mobility Program

  • CIC officials will monitor the way in which compliance and enforcement activities are undertaken. To that end, CIC will continue to engage stakeholders through various outreach activities to explain program changes and to better understand how some sectors use the International Mobility Program.

Application Processing under the Express Entry System

  • CIC will monitor the application process under the Express Entry system to ensure that the six month service standard is met or has exceeded for 80% of the cases. CIC will actively solicit client feedback on this system, and will update web-based manuals and other tools as required so that the system remains responsive, easy to use, and efficient.
  • CIC will assess the feasibility of expanding automated electronic application systems to other business lines, so as to enhance the provision of web-based advice to clients, and introduce broader system efficiencies.

Online Feedback

  • As indicated above, CIC will continue to monitor regs@cic.gc.ca to consider and respond to any future feedback from Canadian businesses on CIC’s regulatory regime and suggestions for improvement.
